\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting degrades sharply with problem complexity, and prior work suggests reasoning traces are pattern replications rather than genuine inference. We ask whether Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) can fix this degradation, and if so, whether the improvement reflects reasoning or pattern matching. We evaluate four interventions---step-level preference pairs, multi-domain DPO, a matched-data diagnostic pair (Synthetic-CoT DPO with correct intermediates vs.\ Incorrect-CoT DPO with scrambled intermediates preserving the correct final answer), and symbolic verification at inference---on LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B across PEMDAS and CoinFlip. Multi-Domain DPO yields modest PEMDAS gains on both models (25.5\%$\to$32.4\% and 32.1\%$\to$34.8\%), but reduces complexity-degradation inconsistently: halving the degradation delta on LLaMA-2-7B while doubling it on Llama 3.1-8B. The diagnostic contrast reveals a model-size interaction: on LLaMA-2-7B, Synthetic-CoT DPO outperforms Incorrect-CoT DPO by 6.5 percentage points (31.0\% vs.\ 24.5\%), suggesting correct intermediates provide some training signal at this scale; on Llama 3.1-8B the gap narrows to 1.3 pp (33.4\% vs.\ 32.1\%), consistent with the pattern-matching interpretation. Symbolic verification achieves 100\% accuracy regardless of the generating model, indicating the bottleneck is in execution, not learning. Faithfulness---the fraction of instances where every intermediate step is symbolically correct \emph{and} the final answer is correct---remains below 5\% across all variants. These results suggest hybrid neuro-symbolic approaches are a more promising path forward than preference optimization alone.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting is the dominant method for eliciting step-by-step reasoning from large language models~\cite{wei2022cot}. However, recent work has established two troubling findings: CoT accuracy degrades sharply as problem complexity increases~\cite{stechly2024thoughtlessness}, and reasoning traces are unfaithful pattern replications rather than genuine logical inference~\cite{zhao2024mirage}. Stechly et al.\ showed that GPT-4 Turbo accuracy on Last Letter Concatenation drops from ${\sim}88\%$ at 1--3 words to under 6\% at 18--20 words with manual CoT, while incorrect CoT traces achieve nearly identical performance to correct ones. Separately, Zhao et al.\ demonstrated through controlled distribution experiments that CoT achieves 100\% accuracy in-distribution but collapses catastrophically under distribution shift, with reasoning traces decoupled from final answers.

A natural hypothesis is that preference optimization---teaching models to prefer correct reasoning paths via Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)~\cite{rafailov2023dpo}---should address these failures. Zhang et al.\ proposed Chain of Preference Optimization (CPO)~\cite{zhang2024cpo}, using Tree-of-Thought search to construct step-level preference pairs. This paper investigates whether DPO-based preference optimization can fix CoT degradation, and if so, whether the improvement reflects genuine reasoning or merely better pattern matching. We investigate four interventions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Step-level preference pairs}: DPO at each intermediate reasoning step, not just the final answer, forcing the model to attend to the logical content of each step.
  \item \textbf{Multi-domain preference data}: Training on preference pairs from both evaluated domains (PEMDAS and CoinFlip) to test whether cross-domain failure is a data coverage issue.
  \item \textbf{Diagnostic ablation (Incorrect-CoT DPO)}: Training on pairs with correct final answers but scrambled intermediate steps, to separate reasoning improvement from pattern-matching improvement.
  \item \textbf{Symbolic verification at inference}: Pairing neural CoT with a symbolic step-level verifier, testing whether the bottleneck is in learning or execution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{CoT prompting and its limitations.} Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022cot} introduced CoT prompting, demonstrating that step-by-step reasoning improves LLM performance on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic tasks. However, Stechly et al.~\cite{stechly2024thoughtlessness} showed that CoT only helps with extremely specific prompts and fails to generalize beyond demonstrated step counts. Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2024mirage} provided a theoretical framework showing CoT generalization is bounded by distribution discrepancy, and that reasoning traces are pattern replications decoupled from answers.

\textbf{Preference optimization for LLMs.} Rafailov et al.~\cite{rafailov2023dpo} proposed DPO as a stable alternative to RLHF. Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2024cpo} extended this to CoT reasoning with CPO, using ToT search trees to construct step-level preference pairs and training with DPO without ground-truth labels.

\textbf{Neuro-symbolic approaches.} Hybrid systems that combine neural generation with symbolic verification have shown promise in mathematical reasoning~\cite{hu2022lora}. Our symbolic verification experiments test whether external verification can address the execution bottleneck that preference optimization alone cannot.

\section{Background and Preliminaries}

\subsection{Complexity-Scaling Evaluation}

Following Stechly et al., we evaluate CoT accuracy as a function of problem complexity $k$ (number of reasoning steps). The \textit{degradation delta} is:
\[
  \Delta = \bar{a}_{\text{hard}} - \bar{a}_{\text{easy}}
\]
where $\bar{a}_{\text{easy}}$ and $\bar{a}_{\text{hard}}$ are mean accuracies over the bottom and top thirds of complexity levels. A negative $\Delta$ indicates degradation; $\Delta \approx 0$ indicates robustness.

\subsection{DPO Objective}

Given preference pairs $(x, y_w, y_l)$ where $y_w$ is preferred over $y_l$, DPO optimizes:
\[
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)\right]
\]
We use LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} (rank 8, $\alpha=16$) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning on Apple Silicon MPS.

\subsection{Baseline Findings}

As a baseline, we first evaluate a simplified whole-trace DPO: the base model is sampled with temperature-based diversity on PEMDAS, correct traces are paired against incorrect ones, and the model is fine-tuned with DPO and LoRA. This establishes whether naive preference optimization affects degradation curves before applying the targeted interventions described in Section~4.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Step-Level Preference Pairs}

Instead of pairing entire correct vs.\ incorrect traces, we create preference pairs at each intermediate reasoning step $i$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Chosen}: correct step $i$ given correct prefix steps $1 \ldots i{-}1$
  \item \textbf{Rejected}: incorrect step $i$ given the same correct prefix
\end{itemize}
For PEMDAS, step correctness is \textit{symbolically verified} by evaluating intermediate arithmetic expressions with Python's \texttt{eval()}, eliminating reliance on the model's own judgment. We sample 8 traces per instance at temperature 0.8 and extract step-level pairs from instances with 4--10 operations.

\subsection{Multi-Domain DPO}

We generate preference pairs for both evaluated benchmark domains (PEMDAS and CoinFlip) using the same temperature-sampling approach: 6 samples per instance, correct vs.\ incorrect pairing. The combined dataset is used for a single DPO training run, testing whether cross-domain data addresses the transfer failure. LastLetterConcatenation is excluded for the same reason it is excluded from evaluation (see Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

\subsection{Diagnostic Ablation: Synthetic vs.\ Incorrect-CoT DPO}
\label{sec:method-diagnostic}

Following the incorrect-CoT methodology of Stechly et al., we compare a correct-reasoning DPO variant against one whose chosen traces have scrambled intermediate steps but the same final answer. Because LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B rarely emit multi-step arithmetic traces under zero-shot CoT (typical sampled outputs are one-line responses like ``The answer is 9''), model-sampled chosen traces contain almost no intermediate content for a corruption procedure to scramble. We therefore synthesise chosen traces algorithmically to guarantee substantive intermediate steps.

\textbf{Synthetic chosen traces.} For each PEMDAS instance we parse the expression from the prompt and reduce it left-to-right using Python: innermost parentheses first, then a left-to-right pass respecting precedence. Each reduction becomes one line in the form ``$a\ \textrm{op}\ b = c$'', followed by a final ``\texttt{Answer: <value>}'' line. This yields chosen traces with a mean of 6.5 intermediate arithmetic steps ($\geq 2$ guaranteed by construction).

\textbf{Rejected traces.} Same stepwise format as chosen but with the final declared answer perturbed by a random integer offset in $\{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3\}$. Both variants therefore share the same rejected distribution; the only controlled difference between the correct-CoT and incorrect-CoT variants is whether the chosen trace's intermediate steps are correct.

\textbf{Corruption procedure.} For each arithmetic line of a chosen trace we independently apply one of three perturbations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{swap\_operands}: swap $a$ and $b$; skipped when the operator is commutative ($+$, $*$) so the swap is never a semantic no-op.
  \item \textbf{wrong\_op}: replace the operator with a different one that, when applied to $a$ and $b$, yields a value different from the declared $c$ (verified by evaluation).
  \item \textbf{wrong\_result}: perturb the declared $c$ by a random offset in $\{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3\}$ (integer-valued traces) or $\pm 1$ (float results). \textit{Disabled on the last arithmetic line so the declared final value is preserved.}
\end{itemize}
A fallback retries alternate strategies if the first pick fails; we verify that every chosen trace has at least one intermediate step actually corrupted. Pairs whose chosen trace contains zero arithmetic lines (e.g., for generated rather than synthesised traces) are dropped. Under this procedure, all 100 synthesised PEMDAS chosen traces are corrupted, with all 650 intermediate steps perturbed (100\% coverage), while final ``\texttt{Answer:}'' lines are preserved verbatim.

If DPO trained on the corrupted pairs achieves accuracy comparable to DPO trained on the correct synthetic pairs, the improvement is attributable to pattern matching rather than to learning correct step-by-step reasoning.

\subsection{Symbolic Verification at Inference}

We implement two modes of symbolic verification:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Verify-only}: Generate CoT normally, then check each step symbolically and report faithfulness.
  \item \textbf{Verify-and-correct}: If a step is symbolically incorrect, substitute the correct value before continuing.
\end{itemize}

For PEMDAS, each step is verified via \texttt{eval()}. For CoinFlip, coin state is tracked programmatically. For LastLetter, last characters are extracted symbolically.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\textbf{Models and Hardware.} We evaluate two base models: LLaMA-2-7B~\cite{touvron2023llama2} (6.74B parameters) and Llama 3.1-8B~\cite{grattafiori2024llama3} (8.03B parameters), both in fp16 on Apple Silicon M5 Max (128GB). Llama 3.1-8B was trained on 15T tokens (vs 2T for LLaMA-2-7B) with an improved tokenizer, providing a stronger baseline to test whether our findings generalize across model capability levels. LoRA rank 8, 3 epochs, batch size 1 with gradient accumulation 4.

\textbf{Benchmarks.} 570 subsampled instances across two domains: PEMDAS (290, steps 2--30, 29 complexity levels) and CoinFlip (280, 1--29 flips, 28 complexity levels), with 10 instances per complexity level. Preliminary evaluation indicated that LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B with zero-shot CoT fail to produce parseable responses on LastLetterConcatenation even at 1--3 words, where GPT-4 Turbo with manual CoT achieves ${\sim}88\%$~\cite{stechly2024thoughtlessness}. We exclude it from the main evaluation as the resulting accuracy floor is uninformative for measuring DPO-induced changes.

\textbf{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Overall accuracy and degradation delta $\Delta$ (standard)
  \item \textbf{Step-level accuracy}: fraction of intermediate steps that are symbolically correct
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness score}: percentage of instances where all steps are correct \textit{and} the final answer is correct
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Experimental Conditions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Base model (direct and CoT), for both LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B
  \item Multi-Domain DPO (PEMDAS + CoinFlip preference pairs; naturalistic model-sampled traces, tests cross-domain transfer)
  \item Step-Level DPO (per-step symbolic ground truth pairs)
  \item Synthetic-CoT DPO (synthesised multi-step PEMDAS traces with correct intermediates)
  \item Incorrect-CoT DPO (same synthesised traces with corrupted intermediates, correct final answer; diagnostic paired with 4)
  \item Symbolic verification: $\{$Base, DPO$\} \times \{$no verification, verify-and-correct$\}$
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} presents zero-shot CoT accuracy and degradation delta $\Delta$ for all variants across both base models. Multi-Domain DPO improves PEMDAS CoT accuracy on both models: from 25.5\% to 32.4\% on LLaMA-2-7B and from 32.1\% to 34.8\% on Llama 3.1-8B. However, CoinFlip accuracy shows no consistent improvement, hovering near chance (50\%) across all variants, and CoinFlip $\Delta$ values are positive (accuracy slightly higher on hard than easy instances), which---given the near-chance overall accuracy---we interpret as noise rather than signal.

The $\Delta$ column reveals a pattern not visible from overall accuracy alone. On LLaMA-2-7B, Multi-Domain DPO reduces degradation relative to base ($\Delta=-13.3$ vs.\ $-27.8$). On Llama 3.1-8B, however, Multi-Domain DPO \emph{worsens} degradation ($\Delta=-21.1$ vs.\ $-10.0$ for base): its 2.7-pp overall gain is driven almost entirely by easy problems, with hard-instance accuracy unchanged. DPO on naturalistic preference data therefore does not uniformly improve the harder end of the complexity curve, and in one case its overall-accuracy gains coincide with \emph{increased} complexity-sensitivity.

The diagnostic pair reveals a model-size interaction. On LLaMA-2-7B, Synthetic-CoT DPO (31.0\%) outperforms Incorrect-CoT DPO (24.5\%) by 6.5 pp, and Incorrect-CoT DPO exhibits substantially worse degradation ($\Delta=-30.0$ vs.\ $-14.4$), suggesting that correct intermediate steps provide meaningful training signal at this scale. On Llama 3.1-8B, the gap narrows to 1.3 pp (33.4\% vs.\ 32.1\%) with comparable degradation ($\Delta=-13.3$ vs.\ $-11.1$), consistent with the pattern-matching interpretation: the stronger model derives little additional benefit from correct intermediates.

\subsection{Diagnostic Ablation: Synthetic-CoT vs.\ Incorrect-CoT DPO}
\label{sec:diagnostic}

The central diagnostic compares two DPO runs that differ only in the correctness of intermediate reasoning steps in their chosen traces: Synthetic-CoT DPO (correct step-by-step arithmetic, correct final answer) and Incorrect-CoT DPO (scrambled intermediate steps as described in §\ref{sec:method-diagnostic}, correct final answer). Rejected traces are identical across the two variants (same format, wrong final answer), so any performance gap must be attributable to intermediate-step correctness during training.

On LLaMA-2-7B, Synthetic-CoT DPO achieves 31.0\% and Incorrect-CoT DPO achieves 24.5\%---a gap of \textbf{6.5 percentage points}. Degradation is also substantially worse for Incorrect-CoT ($\Delta=-30.0$ vs.\ $-14.4$), indicating that on the smaller model, correct intermediate steps provide meaningful training signal beyond output format. On Llama 3.1-8B, the gap narrows to \textbf{1.3 percentage points} (33.4\% vs.\ 32.1\%) with comparable degradation ($\Delta=-13.3$ vs.\ $-11.1$). This is consistent with the pattern-matching interpretation at this scale: the stronger model derives negligible benefit from intermediate-step correctness.

Faithfulness remains uniformly low across both diagnostic variants (5--7 out of 290 on both models), confirming that even where correct intermediates improve accuracy, the model is not learning to produce faithful step-by-step reasoning.

Table~\ref{tab:symbolic} presents the 2$\times$2 comparison between neural CoT and symbolic verification. Symbolic verification achieves 100\% accuracy on both PEMDAS and CoinFlip, regardless of which model variant generates the CoT trace. This indicates that the bottleneck lies in \textit{execution} rather than \textit{learning}: the problems are solvable with deterministic computation, and the neural model's failure appears to be in carrying out the step-by-step procedure rather than in recognizing problem structure.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{DPO diagnostic: pattern matching vs.\ reasoning.} The central diagnostic (§\ref{sec:diagnostic}) reveals a model-size interaction. On LLaMA-2-7B, correct intermediates matter: Synthetic-CoT DPO outperforms Incorrect-CoT DPO by 6.5 pp (31.0\% vs.\ 24.5\%) with a substantially flatter degradation curve ($\Delta=-14.4$ vs.\ $-30.0$). On Llama 3.1-8B, the gap collapses to 1.3 pp (33.4\% vs.\ 32.1\%) with comparable degradation, consistent with the pattern-matching interpretation. One possible explanation is that the stronger model's pretraining already captures enough arithmetic structure that DPO's marginal effect reduces to format learning, while the weaker model still has headroom to extract signal from correct intermediate steps. In both cases, however, faithfulness remains below 2.5\%, so even where correct intermediates improve accuracy, the model is not learning to reason faithfully.

\textbf{Multi-Domain DPO and complexity scaling.} On naturalistic (model-sampled) preference data, Multi-Domain DPO produces mixed effects on the complexity curve: on LLaMA-2-7B it halves the degradation delta ($\Delta = -27.8 \to -13.3$), while on Llama 3.1-8B it doubles it ($\Delta = -10.0 \to -21.1$) despite raising overall accuracy by 2.7 pp. The stronger model's overall-accuracy gain comes almost entirely from easy instances; hard-instance accuracy is unchanged. This indicates that cross-domain DPO on natural outputs does not uniformly improve harder reasoning and, on the stronger base, concentrates its effect on short problems the model could already sometimes solve.

\textbf{Symbolic verification exposes the execution bottleneck.} No DPO variant on either model exceeds 34.8\% on PEMDAS, while symbolic verification achieves 100\% on every variant. The problems are solvable with deterministic computation, suggesting the neural model's failure is predominantly in carrying out step-by-step arithmetic rather than in recognizing problem structure.

\textbf{Step-level preferences remain data starved.} Even Llama 3.1-8B produces only 15 step-level preference pairs (vs 8 for LLaMA-2-7B). While the stronger model produces more diversity, both counts remain insufficient for meaningful preference optimization. True CPO-style training may require models significantly larger than 8B parameters to generate adequate step-level signal.

\textbf{Limitations.} Two models were evaluated (LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B); larger models (70B+) may exhibit qualitatively different behavior. Two benchmark domains were tested; CoinFlip accuracy remains within sampling noise of chance (47--53\%) across all variants on both models, and we therefore treat CoinFlip $\Delta$ values as noise rather than signal in our interpretation. Step-level analysis is limited to PEMDAS where step boundaries are unambiguous. The diagnostic result rests on synthetic chosen traces because natural model outputs at this scale rarely contain multi-step arithmetic to corrupt; the claim therefore concerns DPO on the synthetic-trace distribution rather than on arbitrary naturalistic CoT. All reported numbers come from a single training run per variant on each base model; we do not report confidence intervals, so the 6.5 pp gap on LLaMA-2-7B should be read as suggestive rather than conclusive. All experiments ran on Apple Silicon MPS.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We investigated four targeted interventions to address Chain-of-Thought degradation on two open models (LLaMA-2-7B and Llama 3.1-8B). Multi-Domain DPO on naturalistic preference data produces mixed effects on the complexity curve---halving the degradation delta on LLaMA-2-7B but doubling it on Llama 3.1-8B despite a small overall-accuracy gain---indicating that cross-domain DPO does not uniformly improve harder reasoning. The central diagnostic reveals a model-size interaction: on LLaMA-2-7B, correct intermediate steps provide meaningful training signal (6.5 pp gap over corrupted traces), while on Llama 3.1-8B the gap narrows to 1.3 pp, consistent with pattern matching. Crucially, faithfulness remains below 5\% across all variants on both models, so even where correct intermediates improve accuracy, the model is not learning faithful reasoning. Symbolic verification eliminates degradation entirely (100\% accuracy on all variants), indicating the bottleneck lies in step-by-step execution rather than problem recognition. These results suggest that preference optimization alone cannot bridge the gap between pattern matching and genuine reasoning, and that hybrid neuro-symbolic approaches offer a more promising path forward.
